H3: What is an Enduring Power of Attorney and do I need one?
An Enduring Power of Attorney (EPA) appoints someone to manage your financial and legal affairs if you lose capacity. Unlike a general power of attorney, it remains valid even after you can no longer make decisions. Without one, your family may need to apply to VCAT for a guardianship or administration order — an expensive, slow process. We recommend everyone have an EPA alongside their Will.

H3: What is a Medical Treatment Decision Maker and how is it different from a Power of Attorney?
A Medical Treatment Decision Maker (MTDM) is the person authorised to make medical decisions on your behalf if you're unable to do so yourself. It's entirely separate from an Enduring Power of Attorney, which covers financial matters only. If you haven't formally appointed an MTDM, Victorian law determines who fills the role based on a statutory hierarchy — which may not be the person you'd choose.

H3: Should I update my Will if I buy a property?
Yes. Buying property can significantly affect how your estate is distributed, particularly if it changes the relative value of assets between beneficiaries, or if the property is held in a specific ownership structure. We recommend reviewing your estate plan any time you acquire or sell significant property.

H3: Can superannuation be left in my Will?
Generally, no. Superannuation does not form part of your estate and cannot be directed by your Will. Instead, you direct your super to beneficiaries through a binding death benefit nomination with your fund. Getting this wrong means your super may go to an unintended recipient or be taxed more heavily. We advise on how to align your super nominations with your broader estate plan.

H3: What are my responsibilities as an executor?
As executor, you're responsible for locating the Will, identifying all assets and liabilities, applying for probate where required, paying the deceased's debts and final tax obligations, and distributing the estate to beneficiaries. You can be held personally liable for mismanagement. Having a deceased estate lawyer guide you through each step protects both you and the beneficiaries.

H3: What if beneficiaries disagree or someone wants to contest the Will?
Will disputes are more common than most families expect. In Victoria, grounds include lack of testamentary capacity, undue influence, or inadequate provision under Part IV of the Administration and Probate Act 1958. Time limits apply, so acting promptly matters. We can assess the strength of a claim, advise on realistic outcomes, and work toward resolution without unnecessary court proceedings where possible.

H3: What happens when an estate includes assets in multiple states?
Estates that include property in more than one Australian state require separate probate applications in each relevant jurisdiction. The process and costs vary by state. We handle the Victorian application and can coordinate with solicitors in other states to avoid duplication and keep the estate moving.

H3: What is a Transmission Application and when is it used?
A Transmission Application is the legal process used to transfer real property from a deceased person's name to an executor or beneficiary without a full conveyance. It's handled through Land Use Victoria and is a separate step from probate. If the estate includes real property in Victoria, this is one of the steps we manage as part of the administration process.

H3: What documents are needed to apply for probate in Victoria?
You'll generally need the original Will, the death certificate, an inventory of assets and liabilities, and the completed Supreme Court application forms. The estate may also need to be advertised in the Victoria Government Gazette. Missing or incorrect documentation delays the application. We prepare the full package so the first filing is ready to be accepted.

H3: Can an executor be removed or replaced?
Yes, in limited circumstances. If an executor is unwilling to act, has died, or is found to be acting contrary to the interests of the estate, an application can be made to the Supreme Court for removal or substitution. This is a serious step requiring a clear legal basis. We advise on whether there are sufficient grounds before any application is made.

H3: Can family members object to a probate application?
Interested parties can lodge a caveat with the Supreme Court to prevent a grant being made — for example, if they dispute the validity of the Will or the executor's authority. A caveat triggers a process where both sides must be heard. If you receive notice of a caveat, or want to lodge one, we can advise on your options and represent you through the process.

H3: Can estate assets be distributed before probate is granted?
No. Executors cannot safely distribute estate assets to beneficiaries before probate is granted, as they could be held personally liable for losses resulting from early distribution. Certain urgent steps — such as paying funeral expenses or maintaining the deceased's property — are permitted. We advise on what you can and cannot do while the application is pending.

H3: What is a make-good clause and should I be concerned about it?
A make-good clause requires a tenant to restore the premises to their original state at the end of the lease, which can mean removing fit-out, repainting, replacing flooring, or repairing walls. The cost can be substantial after a long tenancy. We review make-good clauses carefully and often negotiate more practical alternatives — such as cash settlements or a reduced scope of works — before the lease is signed.

H3: What should I check before assigning a commercial lease?
Lease assignment involves transferring your lease obligations to a new tenant, usually as part of a business sale. You need landlord consent, and the lease may impose conditions on who can take it over. Importantly, you may remain personally liable if the incoming tenant defaults, unless the landlord formally releases you. We review the assignment deed, advise on ongoing exposure, and make sure the process is documented correctly.

H3: Who is responsible for outgoings in a commercial lease?
This depends entirely on the lease terms. Some leases require tenants to pay all outgoings — rates, water, insurance, maintenance — while others limit this to specific costs. Many commercial leases describe outgoings broadly, which can lead to unexpected bills during the annual reconciliation. We identify and explain all outgoings provisions before you sign so there are no surprises.

H3: What options do I have if I need to exit a commercial lease early?
Breaking a commercial lease before its end date can expose you to significant liability, including rent until the premises are re-let and the landlord's re-letting costs. Options may include subletting (if the lease permits), negotiating a surrender with the landlord, or exercising any early termination clause. We review your lease and explain your realistic options, including the likely cost of each path.
